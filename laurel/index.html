Douglas Engelbart was born in 1925. By the time he became a major developer of computer science in the 1960’s, computers were barely accessible to the public. They were used mainly for computing, processing, and storing information. He was the middle child in a family of three from Portland, Oregon. His father passed away when he was 9 years old, and halfway through his college education in Corvallis, he was drafted into the Navy. During this time, he read the article “As We May Think” by Vannevar Bush. This article had a great impact on his life philosophy and he went to on to be one of computer science’s greatest contributors.

After the Navy he finished a bachelor’s degree in electrical engineering and then went on to get a PhD and M.S. the same field. Engelbart knew that information was already analyzed and stored by computers. He reasoned that the capacity for problem solving could be increased greatly by compiling existing knowledge; integrating information with technology to make it universal. In 1957 he went to work on his ideas at the Stanford Research Institute for the next 20 years. During this time at SRI, he developed his “Program on Human Effectiveness” which says,

“Our aim is to bring significant improvement to the real-life problem-solving effectiveness of individuals.”
This program directly evolved concepts such as “augmenting human intellect, improvement infrastructure, co-evolution of artifacts with social-cultural language-practices, and bootstrapping.” His lab pioneered “an elaborate hypermedia-groupware system called NLS (for oN-Line System)” (douglasengelbart.org) Through this online system, integrated prototypes of renown technological firsts where operational. Breakthroughs such as: mouse, display editing, windows, cross-file editing, outline processing, hypermedia, and groupware. dougengelbart.org

Perhaps his most famous accomplishment is coming up with the first concept of the computer mouse as an interactive tool for the computer. He received over 60 awards for his lifetime of work in Computer Science. Some of the more prestigious of these include the National Medal of Technology, the Lemelson-MIT Prize, and ACM's 1997 A.M. Turing Award.

Douglas Engelbart was a hardworking dreamer. Some more interesting anecdotes found about him on his website are below: “When Doug was growing up, he was poor and milked the cow before he walked across several cow pastures to get to school in his dirty boots - you can imagine how he might have been teased! And then, as an adult, his ideas were so different from the rest of his peers, he was told over and over that he was wrong, crazy, unacceptable, but he knew inside that he was onto something important, so he thought of this quote ‘The rate at which a person can mature is directly proportional to the amount of embarrassment he can tolerate.’ ” Sounds like Engelbart knew all about hard work and overcoming adversity. The next quote really illustrates that he had an incredible imagination, with the brains to achieve his visions “ Since he had studied in college to be an engineer, and had been reading about the first computers, and had been trained on fancy new stuff called radar in World War II, he figured he could put that all together into a way that problem solvers could sit at "workingstations" and fly around their information space working on solutions together.”
